# The Cost of Range

Range sounds attractive until you actually have to live inside it. From the outside, people often see the upside first: adaptability, flexibility, broader capability, the ability to connect different parts of a system. Those things are real. But range also has a cost, and I think it is worth naming that clearly.

## Why the cost exists

The moment you stop building your whole identity inside one narrow discipline, you lose some of the simplicity that comes with that discipline. Your story gets messier. Your path gets harder to summarize. Other people understand you more slowly. You have more edges to sharpen and fewer excuses for staying static.

That is the price of refusing a smaller box.

## The risk of looking unfocused

One of the obvious risks is being seen as unfocused. People often trust narrow expertise because it is easy to recognize. Broad capability is harder to read. It can look indecisive from the outside even when it is highly intentional from the inside.

I know that risk is real. I do not dismiss it. But for me, the answer is not to narrow artificially just to look cleaner. The answer is to make the range coherent enough that the work itself explains it.

## The permanent learning burden

Another cost is that range keeps the learning curve alive. There is no stable resting point where I get to declare the system finished and live inside that declaration forever. New work exposes new gaps. Larger outcomes demand stronger integration. If I want the benefits of range, I have to accept the burden of staying in motion.

That can be tiring. It can also be psychologically demanding, because it forces me to keep confronting what I still do not know.

## Why I still choose it

I still choose range because narrowing too early feels worse. The things I want to build require multiple surfaces to hold together. Pretending that one domain can explain the whole project would make my identity cleaner and my work weaker.

So this is one of those trade-offs I accept consciously. I accept the possibility of being misread. I accept the longer learning path. I accept the pressure of broader responsibility. I accept the ambiguity, because it still feels more truthful than reducing myself to one lane that cannot contain the actual work.

## Cost as proof of seriousness

In a strange way, I think the cost is part of what makes the identity real. If there were no burden in it, it would probably just be aesthetic. The fact that range asks more from me is part of what keeps it honest.
